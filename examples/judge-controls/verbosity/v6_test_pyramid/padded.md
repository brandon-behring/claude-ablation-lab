## Unit vs integration tests — and the pyramid

Testing strategy is a subject on which much ink has been spilled, and the unit-versus-integration distinction is the right place to begin, so let us begin there and work our way up carefully. **Unit tests** are tests that exercise one component in isolation, which is achieved by replacing the component's collaborators with fakes or stubs of one kind or another. The virtues of this arrangement are considerable and worth listing one by one: unit tests are fast, running in milliseconds; they are deterministic, giving the same answer every time; and they are precise, in the sense that when one fails, the failure points directly at the broken unit by name. **Integration tests**, on the other hand, exercise real components operating together — a service talking to a real database, say, or two modules communicating across a seam — and while they are undeniably slower and undeniably flakier than unit tests, they earn their place by catching the entire class of problems that unit tests are structurally incapable of catching: wiring errors, schema drift, serialization mismatches, transaction semantics, and everything else that lives between components rather than inside them.

This brings us to the **testing pyramid**, the famous heuristic that everyone has seen drawn on a whiteboard at some point. The prescription is as follows: many unit tests at the base of the pyramid, a smaller number of integration tests in the middle layer, and just a handful of end-to-end tests at the very top. It is worth pausing to ask why the pyramid has this shape rather than some other, and the answer is economics, plain and simple — both the cost of running a test and its tendency toward flakiness rise with the test's scope, and so the rational strategy is to buy the bulk of your coverage where coverage is cheapest, reserving the expensive, broad tests for precisely those properties that only they are capable of verifying.

One closing caveat, which is important precisely because the pyramid is so often treated as gospel: the pyramid is a heuristic and not a law of nature. Systems whose real risk lives in the composition of parts rather than in the parts themselves — integration-heavy services and data pipelines being the standard examples — can perfectly legitimately shift their testing weight toward the middle layer. The invariant that is actually worth keeping, the principle underneath the picture, is the *reason* for the shape rather than the shape itself: place each behavior's test at the cheapest level of the pyramid that can actually falsify that behavior. Everything else is negotiable.
