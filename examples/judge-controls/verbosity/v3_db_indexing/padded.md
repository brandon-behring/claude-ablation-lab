## When to add a database index — and what it costs

Indexing is one of those topics where the high-level idea is simple but where the details, as always, are what make the difference in practice, so let us go through the whole picture carefully and in order. At the highest level, an index buys you fast lookups on a column, and it does so at the price of slower writes and extra storage — nothing is free, as ever. The time to add one is when a **selective** predicate appears in your hot queries — which is to say in WHERE clauses, in JOIN keys, or in ORDER BY prefixes — and when the query planner, left to its own devices, is otherwise scanning far more rows than it actually ends up returning to you. The classic tell, the thing to look for first, is `EXPLAIN` output showing sequential scans on large tables that produce small result sets; when you see that pattern, an index is usually the answer.

Now to the costs, because the costs are concrete and they are paid continuously. Every single INSERT, UPDATE, and DELETE against the table must also maintain each and every index that exists on it, which means that write-heavy tables pay a per-index tax on every single mutation; it is precisely for this reason that bulk-load jobs so often drop the indexes first and rebuild them afterwards. Beyond the write tax, indexes consume storage on the same order as the data they index, which adds up. And it gets worse: an index can simply go unused, sitting there costing writes and space while contributing nothing — low-selectivity columns, of which booleans and small enums are the usual examples, rarely justify an index at all, for the simple reason that the planner will ignore any index that does not filter much.

Two refinements do disproportionate work here and deserve to be spelled out properly. The first concerns **composite indexes**: these must match the query's leftmost-prefix pattern, meaning that an index on `(a, b)` serves `WHERE a=?` and serves `WHERE a=? AND b=?`, but does not serve `WHERE b=?` on its own — the order of columns in the index is not decorative. The second concerns **covering indexes**: when an index includes every column the query selects, the engine can answer the query entirely from the index without ever touching the table itself, which can be a dramatic win. The discipline that falls out of all of this, stated plainly: index the queries you actually run, verify what the planner does with them, and delete the indexes that nothing uses.
