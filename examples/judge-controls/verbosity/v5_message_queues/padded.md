## At-least-once vs exactly-once delivery

Message delivery semantics are one of those areas where terminology gets thrown around loosely, and where being precise pays off handsomely in interviews and in production alike, so let us be precise, and let us start from the beginning. **At-least-once** delivery is the mode in which the broker redelivers any message that has not been acknowledged within some time window: the guarantee this buys you is that no message is ever lost, but the price — and there is always a price — is that consumers can see duplicates. To see why, consider the failure case: a consumer that crashes after it has processed a message but before it has acknowledged it will, upon recovery, receive that same message again. **At-most-once**, in which the consumer acknowledges before processing, trades in exactly the opposite direction: you will never see a duplicate, but a crash at the wrong moment silently loses the message forever.

Now for the phrase that causes the most confusion in this whole area: exactly-once. Here is the thing that has to be said clearly and up front: true **exactly-once delivery** across an unreliable network is impossible in the general case — this is the two-generals problem in its practical form — because the sender fundamentally cannot distinguish a lost message from a lost acknowledgment. So what is it, then, that real systems actually ship under the "exactly-once" label? What they ship is **exactly-once processing**, which is a different and achievable thing: it consists of at-least-once delivery underneath, combined with deduplication on top. The deduplication can take several forms — idempotent consumers, unique message IDs checked against a set of already-processed IDs, or transactional consume-process-produce pipelines, of which Kafka's mechanism of reading offsets and writing results inside one transaction is the canonical example.

The engineering consequence of all this, the takeaway to actually carry into your designs, is the following: design your consumers to be idempotent from the start, and treat duplicate messages as perfectly normal input rather than as some exceptional error path. Once you have done that, something pleasant happens: the broker's redelivery machinery stops being a hazard you defend against and becomes a correctness feature working in your favor.
