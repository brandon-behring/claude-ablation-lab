## The CAP theorem

The CAP theorem is one of those foundational results that comes up again and again in system design discussions, and it is well worth taking the time to really understand what it does and does not say. At its heart, CAP says that a distributed data store can guarantee at most two of three properties, and — this is the part that is often glossed over, so it bears emphasizing — this constraint applies specifically **while a network partition is happening**. The three properties in question are Consistency, which means that every read sees the latest write; Availability, which means that every request gets a response of some kind; and Partition tolerance, which means that the system survives dropped or delayed messages between nodes.

Now, here is the thing that is important to internalize about all of this. Since real networks do in fact partition — and they really do, more often than one might hope — P is not actually optional in any meaningful sense. What this means is that the actual choice, the real decision that a system designer faces, is the choice between C and A during a partition. To make this concrete, consider the two sides. A CP system, of which a quorum-based store refusing minority-side writes would be an example, returns errors rather than stale data; it would rather fail a request than lie to you. An AP system, on the other hand, of which Dynamo-style stores are the classic example, keeps answering on both sides of the partition and then reconciles the divergence later, after the network has healed.

There are two practical refinements that matter and that are worth spelling out. The first refinement is that the choice is per-operation and not per-system, which is to say that one and the same database can serve linearizable reads on a critical path while serving eventually-consistent reads elsewhere in the application, where staleness is acceptable. The second refinement goes by the name PACELC, and it extends the picture to normal operation: **e**lse — that is, when there is no partition in progress — the trade-off becomes latency versus consistency instead, because coordinating replicas on every single write costs network round-trips even when nothing whatsoever is failing. In summary, then: partitions force the C-versus-A choice, the choice is made operation by operation, and even in the absence of partitions the coordination costs remain.
