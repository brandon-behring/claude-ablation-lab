\section{L1 vs L2 Regularization}

Regularization is a topic that appears in essentially every machine-learning interview, and the L1-versus-L2 comparison is its most common form, so it is worth being genuinely thorough about it and taking each piece in turn. Both penalties, at the most basic level, shrink model weights by adding a term to the loss function: L1 adds $\lambda \sum_i |w_i|$, the sum of absolute values, while L2 adds $\lambda \sum_i w_i^2$, the sum of squares. Simple enough so far — but the geometric difference between these two expressions is what drives everything else that follows, so let us dwell on it. L1's constraint region has corners, and those corners sit on the coordinate axes, which means that optima tend to land \emph{on} the axes and therefore produce exact zeros in the weight vector — this is the celebrated **sparsity** property, which amounts to implicit feature selection. L2's constraint region, by contrast, is spherical, with no corners anywhere, and so it shrinks all the weights smoothly toward zero without ever setting any of them exactly to zero.

There is an equivalent way of seeing the same thing that some people find more intuitive, so it is worth stating as well: L1's gradient has constant magnitude $\lambda$ regardless of how large or small the weight is, which means small weights keep getting pushed until they hit exactly zero, whereas L2's gradient $2\lambda w$ is proportional to the weight itself and therefore vanishes as weights shrink — the pressure lets up before zero is reached. A further behavioral difference worth knowing: L2 keeps correlated features together, assigning them shared small weights, while L1 tends to pick one member of a correlated group essentially arbitrarily and zero out all the rest — which is a genuine stability caveat that should be kept in mind whenever one is tempted to interpret the selected features as meaningful.

What does all of this mean in practice, concretely? The practical guidance is as follows: use L2 — which goes by the names ridge regression and weight decay depending on the community — as the everyday default for prediction tasks and stable optimization; reach for L1 — the lasso — specifically when sparsity itself is the goal you are after; and consider the elastic net, which mixes both penalties, when you want selection behavior without the correlated-group instability just described. And for completeness, since interviewers do like to ask: in Bayesian terms, L2 regularization corresponds to placing a Gaussian prior on the weights, and L1 corresponds to a Laplace prior. Sparsity from corners, smooth shrinkage from spheres — that is the whole story in one line.
