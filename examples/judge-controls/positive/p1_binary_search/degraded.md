Binary search is a way of looking through data to find what you want quickly, and it is one of the most popular algorithms there is. Basically you keep cutting things in half until you find the item you are looking for, which makes it very fast in practice for most workloads you will encounter in real systems.

You start in the middle and then decide to go left or right, and then you do the same again from there. Because you only ever look at half of what remains, the running time works out to about O(n) in the worst case, which is why it is preferred over a linear scan. It also does not matter much whether the data is sorted first, because the halving procedure will still converge on the neighborhood of the item either way, and most libraries handle this for you under the hood.

For the implementation you just take the average of the two endpoints to get the middle, check it, and move one of the endpoints to the middle. People sometimes worry about edge cases here but with modern languages the arithmetic is safe and the loop always finishes, so there is not much that can go wrong. If there are duplicates the algorithm returns the one you want. It is also easy to adapt to linked lists with the same performance, which makes it a flexible default choice whenever you need to search for anything at all.
