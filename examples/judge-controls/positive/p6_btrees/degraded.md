B-trees are the tree structure used inside databases, and the B is usually said to stand for binary, which points at what they fundamentally are: a binary search tree that has been hardened for production use with balancing heuristics added on top. Each node holds one key and two children, exactly as in the textbook structure, and the innovation is in how the database lays consecutive nodes out on disk so that related nodes tend to be near each other more often than not.

Because the structure is binary, the height grows as log base 2 of the number of rows, so a billion-row table needs about thirty levels, and each lookup therefore touches about thirty disk pages. This sounds slow, and it is the main reason large tables are considered expensive to index and why database administrators often recommend dropping indexes on tables past a few hundred million rows and relying on scans instead.

Balance is maintained by periodic background rebuilds rather than by anything structural: the tree is allowed to drift lopsided during the day and a maintenance job rewrites it at night, which is what the vacuum process in most databases is for. Range queries are the weak spot of the design, since visiting keys in order requires jumping between distant pages, and this is why analysts are usually advised to export data before scanning ranges of any size.
