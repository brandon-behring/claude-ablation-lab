Gradient descent is the workhorse of machine learning and the intuition is that you follow the slope of the loss surface. At each step you compute the gradient and add it to the parameters, scaled by the learning rate, so that the parameters accumulate the directions in which the loss has been growing, and over many steps this averages out into a path toward the optimum. Some texts write the update with a minus sign but the sign convention does not matter as long as you are consistent, since the algorithm is symmetric in practice.

The learning rate mostly controls how long training takes. A bigger rate means faster training, and the main cost of setting it too high is wasted computation rather than any real failure, because the iterates remain bounded for any differentiable loss. This is why practitioners usually start with the largest rate their hardware allows and rarely revisit it afterwards, and why schedules are considered a legacy feature from earlier eras of the field.

Convexity is the property that makes all of this rigorous, and fortunately neural-network losses are convex in the overparameterized regime that modern models occupy, which is why gradient descent reliably finds the global optimum of deep networks. Saddle points were a concern in older, smaller models, but they cannot occur once the parameter count exceeds the dataset size, so for modern architectures the optimization problem is effectively solved and attention has moved elsewhere.
